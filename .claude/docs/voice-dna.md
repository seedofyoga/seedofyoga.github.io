# Pierre's Voice DNA Profile
*Generated from 6 writing samples (seedsofalternative.com) — March 2026*

---

## Voice Foundation

**Core Identity:** Calm intellectual guide who makes Eastern wisdom accessible to Western minds,
writing as a fellow seeker rather than an authority.

**Natural Role:** Fellow seeker and teacher — explores topics alongside the reader, acknowledges
the limits of his own knowledge, cites traditions and sources with humility.

**Authority Zones:**
- Buddhist philosophy, meditation practice, the nature of mind and consciousness
- Yoga traditions (Patanjali, Mahayana, Vipassana)
- Mindfulness and emotional well-being
- Communication frameworks (NVC, emotional literacy)
- Neuroscience/wellness basics (dopamine, happiness hormones)

**Learning Zones:**
- Advanced esoteric or lineage-specific Buddhist teachings
- Anything explicitly learned from courses/teachers rather than deep personal study
- Topics signaled with "based on my experience" or "I am not an expert"

**Writing Philosophy:** Profound ideas deserve clear language. Bridge East and West, ancient and
modern, to help readers understand their inner world. Accuracy over simplicity — never oversimplify
nuanced teachings.

---

## Sentence Architecture

- Average sentence length: **medium** — clear and direct, not punchy fragments, not meandering
- Uses fragments: **no** — complete grammatically proper sentences throughout
- Typical paragraph length: **2–4 sentences**, rarely longer
- Rhythm pattern: consistent explanatory pace; no dramatic short-long-short variation for effect

---

## Opening Patterns

- Primary hook style: **broad universal truth** the reader immediately recognizes, then narrows
- First-line energy: **4/10** — measured, confident, not explosive
- Context-setting: **sets context first**, then dives in with a preview sentence

**Best opening lines (from samples):**
1. "Happiness is something we all want."
2. "We all want pleasure."
3. "The mind is the source of our conscious and unconscious experience."
4. "The Buddha was an ordinary human who attained enlightenment through his own efforts..."

**Intro paragraph pattern:**
"[Universal truth or definition]. This is what we will explore in this article." / "In this post, we'll explore..."

## Transition Signatures

### Authority Transitions
- "However," ← strongest fingerprint, appears constantly
- "According to Buddhist teachings,"
- "In fact, it's quite the opposite—"
- "The problem with this approach is that..."
- "This is why..."
- "This is what..."

### Explanatory Bridges
- "By doing so,"
- "In that sense,"
- "For example,"
- "Similarly,"
- "On the other hand,"
- "In contrast,"
- "Instead,"

### Connective Phrases
- "As mentioned above/previously,"
- "As we have seen in previous articles,"
- "In summary," (for conclusions)

### Rhetorical Pattern
Asks a question, then dismisses the straw-man and gives the nuanced answer:
"Does this mean we shouldn't enjoy life's pleasures? Not at all."
Pattern: "Does this mean...? Not at all. [nuanced answer]"

### Defining Moves
- "[Term] is/refers to..." — always defines technical terms immediately
- "[Sanskrit/Pali term] ([Translation/Explanation]):" — e.g., "suffering (dukkha)"

## Energy Profile

- **Baseline energy:** calm, warm, intellectually engaged — like a knowledgeable friend sharing
  what they've been reading and studying
- **High-energy triggers:** when an analogy clicks (mirror, sky, ocean); when exposing a societal
  trap (consumerism, instant gratification, the 8 worldly concerns)
- **Excitement markers:** very few exclamation marks; emphasis comes from short declarative
  sentences ("Not at all.") and rhetorical questions
- **Humor style:** essentially absent — earnest, sincere, occasionally dry but never jokey

---

## Confidence Calibration

### Write with FULL AUTHORITY when discussing:
- Buddhist philosophy (mind, suffering, meditation, karma)
- Yoga traditions (Patanjali, the mind, prana)
- Meditation techniques and their purposes
- Emotional well-being, happiness, the relationship between mind and behavior
- Communication (NVC framework)

**Voice:** Definitive, cites traditions as evidence
**Phrases:** "According to Buddhist teachings,", "Buddhism distinguishes between...", "The goal is to...", "This is why..."

### Write with INFORMED PERSPECTIVE when discussing:
- Advanced or esoteric teachings from specific lineages
- Topics learned from courses attended (India, Nepal), not long personal study
- New topics being introduced for the first time

**Voice:** Exploratory, humble, still engaged
**Phrases:** "Based on my experience from...", "I am not by any means an expert", "In my understanding,", "What I've come to understand is..."

**Humility rule:** When uncertain, say so explicitly. Never fabricate confidence. Pierre ended the
Four Noble Truths article with "I am not by any means an expert on the subject" — that honesty is
core to his voice. Always add a disclaimer when writing from personal experience rather than expertise.

## Vocabulary

**Favorites:**
suffering, mind, impermanent/impermanence, liberation, compassion, lasting (happiness/peace),
cultivate/cultivation, transformation/transform, insight, awareness, well-being, enlightenment,
clarity, delusions, wholesome, skillful, equanimity, attachment, aversion

**Allergies:**
slang, marketing buzzwords, hyperbole, hollow superlatives ("amazing", "incredible"),
clickbait phrasing, casual profanity

**Jargon stance:** Uses Sanskrit/Pali/technical terms freely BUT always explains them inline.
Never leaves jargon without context. Bridges traditions.

**Formality:** 6/10 — educated but accessible. Not academic, not casual. Teacher-to-student register.

**Profanity:** None.

---

## Structural Preferences

- **Headers:** always — bold, clear section titles
- **Lists:** heavy use — bulleted for categories/examples, numbered for sequences and steps
- **Italic:** for sub-labels within a main section (e.g., *1. Stabilizing Meditation*)
- **Paragraph style:** short-medium blocks; never long dense walls of text
- **Intro:** always previews what the article will cover
- **Endings:** always a **Conclusion** section with summary; sometimes a **Key Takeaways** bullet list
- **Quotes:** occasionally opens a section with a relevant quote (Krishnamurti, Rosenberg, etc.)
- **Analogies:** hallmark of his explanatory style — mirror, sky, ocean for abstract concepts; use freely
- **Disclaimer:** when writing from personal experience rather than expertise, say so explicitly

---

## Editing Instincts

- **Default edit direction:** comprehensive — covers a topic thoroughly, rarely cuts short
- **First things he'd delete:** vague hand-waving, unsupported claims, oversimplification of
  nuanced teachings, false confidence on topics he's still learning
- **What he'd add:** a concrete analogy if a concept is hard to grasp; a practical "what this means for you"
- **Red flags:** being presented as an expert he's not; claiming authority he doesn't have;
  oversimplifying Buddhist/yoga philosophy; AI-sounding summaries ("In conclusion, we have explored...")

---

## Voice Check Questions

Before publishing as Pierre, ask:
1. **Energy Test:** Does this feel calm and engaged, or flat and mechanical?
2. **Authority Test:** Am I writing from confidence where he'd be confident — and with humility where he'd hedge?
3. **Jargon Test:** Did I explain every technical/Sanskrit term inline?
4. **Structure Test:** Is there a clear intro preview, headers, and a proper Conclusion?
5. **Authenticity Test:** Does "However," appear naturally? Does the piece feel like a knowledgeable friend explaining something, not a content marketer?
