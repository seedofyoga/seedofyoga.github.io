## Facebook Post

The first time you hear chanting in a yoga class, it can feel slightly strange. The sounds are unfamiliar. You're not sure whether you're meant to join in, or what the words mean, or where this fits into a session that began with breathing and stretching.

Most of us arrive at yoga through the body. What some of us discover along the way is something older and wider than postures — a philosophy, a set of practices for the mind, and, for some, a relationship with sacred sound.

Mantras are one of those practices. In Sanskrit, the word itself means "a tool for the mind." The idea is that repeating certain sounds or phrases — aloud, in a whisper, or silently — creates a specific quality of attention. Whether you approach this from a traditional standpoint or a more pragmatic one, the basic observation tends to hold: chanting changes how the mind feels.

I wrote a guide to the main mantras used in yoga — Om, the Gayatri Mantra, Om Namah Shivaya, So Hum, and others. What each one means, where it comes from, and why it is still used today.
