## Facebook Post

The tradition we call yoga is several thousand years old. However, most of what happens in a yoga class today — the postures, the sequences, the physical emphasis — emerged in the 20th century. That gap between the ancient roots and the modern form is worth understanding, because it changes how we see our practice.

The history moves through six broad periods. The Vedic rishis who first used the word yoga around 1500 BCE had nothing resembling what we do on the mat. The Bhagavad Gita, around 200 BCE, opened three distinct paths — knowledge, devotion, selfless action — to anyone living an ordinary life. Patanjali, around the 4th century CE, gave yoga its first systematic written framework: 196 aphorisms, and a definition of yoga as the stilling of the mind that is still the most cited in the tradition. It was only from the 9th century that the physical body became central — through Hatha yoga, the Hatha Yoga Pradipika, and eventually the lineage of Krishnamacharya in 20th-century Mysore, from which nearly every major yoga style practiced worldwide today descends.

Wherever you are on the mat, you are part of a very long lineage — one that is still developing.
